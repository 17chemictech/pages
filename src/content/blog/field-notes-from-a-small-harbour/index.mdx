The first useful homepage test is not analytics. It is whether you want to stay on it for a minute longer than necessary.

## Does it feel arranged?

When a page starts to work, it feels arranged instead of assembled. The cards do not just exist; they seem placed. The categories do not just label; they suggest paths.

## Does it make the archive lighter?

One of the best things a front page can do is remove pressure from the archive. The archive no longer has to sell itself in the first screen. It can simply be deep and reliable.

## Does it invite a second action?

The final question is whether a visitor can imagine what to do next. Read a feature? Open a category? Join the list? If that next move is obvious, the page is doing its job.

Useful sites do not just show taste. They help the reader move.
